Argument From Positive/Negative Consequences

The Argument Form Positive/Negative Consequence, is generally used for when we want to know whether to implement some plan or policy but are wondering about the consequences of doing so.

It is structured as the following:

Premise: "Sex education classes lead to increased sexual promiscuity in teenagers. This is bad."

Conclusion: "Sex education classes should not be given to teenagers."

Appeal to Expert Opinion

Appeal to Expert Opinion is used when specialist knowledge is required that is not within our grasp, but maybe within someone else's

It is structured as the following:

Expertise Assumption: "Michael is a PHD lecturer at Kings College London studying the effects of public lockdown restrictions on the number of Covid-19 cases throughout the United Kingdom"

Assertion Premise: "Michael asserts that Lockdowns result in a decrease of Covid-19 cases"

Conclusion: "Lockdowns reduce Covid-19 cases"

Appeal to Popular Opinion

Appeal to Popular Opinion is a general acceptance of a proposition that may count as a reason for believing it to be true.

It is structured as the following:

General Acceptance Premise: "Religious belief is generally accepted as being virtuous"

Presumption Premise: "If most people accept the virtue of religious belief, there is reason for thinking that it may actually be virtuous."

Conclusion: "There is reason for thinking that religious belief is actually virtuous."

Argument from the Position to Know

Argument from the Position to Know

It is structured as the following:

Position to Know: "John is a regular at the Italian Restaurant"

Assertion Premise: "John asserts that the Ceaser Salad is the best meal at the restaurant"

Conclusion: "The Ceaser salad is the best meal at the Restaurant."

Argument from Analogy

Argument from Analogy is used when we lack familiarity with a particular scenario, but we are familiar with another scenario that seems similar.

It is structured as the following:

Similarity Premise: "The relationship between a parent and child can be said to be similar between citizen and state"

Base Premise: "Parents can impose any riles they like on their children"

Conclusion: "The government can impose any rules they like on their citizens"

Argument from Correlation to Cause

Argument from Correlation to Cause is used when we want to know whether one event causes another. How could we tell?

It is structured as the following:

Correlation Premise: "The lack of public restrictions have been seen to increases the number of covid cases"

Conclusion: "Lack of public restrictions cause a higher number of Covid cases"

The Slippery Slope Argument

The Slippery Slope Argument is used when we want know whether or not to implement some policy or plan

It is structured as the following:

First Step Premise: "You decide to eat Junk food for lunch "

Recursive Premise: "Eating junk food may lead to consuming large amounts of unhealthy food"

Bad Outcome Premise: "Eating Junk food regularly can lead to diabetes"

Conclusion: "You should not eat Junk food"

Argument from Sign

Argument from Sign is used when we to infer what the truth about one fact indicates or mean for the truth of a general hypothesis?

It is structured as the following:

Specific Premise: "A man has received a positive result on his Covid test"

General Premise: "A positive result on the covid test indicates the man has Covid"

Conclusion: "The man probably has Covid"

Argument from Commitment

Argument from Commitment is used when we want to understand what we can infer from a person's commitments

It is structured as the following:

Commitment Evidence Premise: "Steven claims to be a Professional Marathon Runner"

Linkage of Commitments Premise: "A Professional Marathon Runner usually believes in keeping fit and healthy"

Conclusion: "Steven Probably believes in keeping fit and healthy"

Argument from Inconsistent Commitment

Argument from Inconsistent Commitment is used when one wants to know if a person's commitments can be incompatible

It is structured as the following:

Initial Commitment Premise: "Sarah claims that she is Pro-Life"

Opposed Commitment Premise: "Sarah recently had an abortion"

Conclusion: "Sarah's prior supporting commitments is conflicting with his initial commitment premise "

DirectAD Hominem Argument

DirectAd Hominem Argument is used when we want to find out if a person's character affects the validity of their argument.

It is structured as the following:

Character Attack Premise: "Trump often cited incorrect and made up facts"

Conclusion "Trumps statements where he quoted facts should not be accepted"

Circumstantial Ad Hominem

Circumstantial Ad Hominem is used when we to know if we can undermine a person's argument by using circumstantial ad hominems?

It is structured as the following:

Argument Premise: "Boris Johnson publicly spoke about marriage and how one should be committed and not break the oath made between one another"

Inconsistent Commitment Premise: "Evidence emarges that Boris Johnson had cheated on his wife"

Credibility Questioning Premise: "Boris Johnsons statements regarding marriage are now undermined due to his actions"

Conclusion: "We can safely ignore Boris Johnsons statements regarding marriage"

Argument from Verbal Classification

Argument from Verbal Classification is used to infer what we can deduce from the fact that something belongs to a general class of objects

It is structured as the following:

Individual Premise: "Mark has blue eyes"

Presumption Premise: "People who have blue eyes would never were colored contact lenses"

Conclusion: "Mark would never wear colored contact lenses"
